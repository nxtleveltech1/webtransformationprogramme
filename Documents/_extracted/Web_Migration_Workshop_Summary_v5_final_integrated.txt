
Web Migration Programme
Workshop Notes, Actions, Risks and Source Pictures
Compiled from the whiteboard sessions and workshop inputs covering Publishing, Go-Live Readiness, Design Systems, Execution, and Cross Channels Solutions.

Executive Summary
Across all workshop areas, the main programme issue is that the work is being described as a migration, while several delivery streams imply a broader transformation: new Design System adoption, V2 templates, information architecture changes, analytics, observability, automated testing, revised publishing workflow and legacy decommissioning. This distinction must be formally resolved because it changes scope, effort, budget, timeline and risk profile.
Programme theme
Current assessment
Publishing
High-risk capacity bottleneck against a large Omar page inventory.
Design System / OMDS
Strategic dependency and governance layer, not merely a UI component library.
Execution
Scope appears to include technical uplift and transformation, not only migration.
Go-live readiness
Requires stronger sequencing across Beta, Staging, Production, ServiceNow, security, communications and decommissioning.
Cross Channels
Delivery progressing but exposed to front-end and BA resource constraints.
Priority Decisions Required
Confirm whether the programme is a content migration, a web transformation, or a hybrid with clearly bounded scope.
Approve a resourcing plan for publishing, front-end development, business analysis and regional support.
Confirm Design System V2 readiness, component gaps, approval governance and exception handling.
Approve environment sequencing across Contentstack, Beta/Staging, Production, Live Testing and Tooling.
Confirm SEO, analytics, observability and ServiceNow ownership before build completion.

Approve a single interconnected lifecycle and progress tracker across Define, Discovery, Design, Approval, Handover, Support and Go Live.
Confirm content governance gates, including content pack readiness, URL audit completion, image standards and switch-off criteria.
Confirm interim Brand / CI approval route and the rollout approach for non-migrated sites and existing live journeys.
Confirm capacity for publishing, copywriting, design volume and cluster support before committing to migration milestones.
1. Publishing Workstream
Area / owner: Publishing
Summary
Publishing is the second-last step in the delivery process and is currently constrained by resource availability, development completion, content readiness, asset sign-off, and approved URL structures. Current publishing capacity presents a material delivery risk given the Omar migration page volume.
Key Notes
Only two publishers are available: one full-time on migration and one supporting BAU across 14 sites, secure sites and application support.
No dedicated publishers are currently identified within Omar, while Wealth has a publisher.
Publishing can only start once Contentstack development is complete and content, assets and URL structures are signed off.
New template pages are estimated at roughly two hours per page; as-is pages are estimated at roughly 30 minutes per page.
Omar page volume was noted as approximately 1,530 pages as of 1 June.
Every page, whether new or as-is, needs to be rebuilt in Beta and tracked through a publishing progress spreadsheet.
Key Risks
Publishing capacity is insufficient for the migration volume.
Publishing is blocked by upstream design, development and content readiness.
Definition of “as-is” remains unclear, including whether it means V2 with new navigation and footer.
Testing cannot begin until all pages are built in Beta.
Recommended Actions
Confirm dedicated publishing resources for Omar.
Define and approve the “as-is” migration scope.
Create a complete page inventory and publishing tracker.
Confirm content, asset and URL readiness dates.
Build a capacity model against the 1,530-page baseline.
Final Update - Content Pack, Asset Standards and Publishing Inputs
Publishing handover must include a complete content pack: final copy, images, link mapping, PDFs / downloadable assets, metadata and any required multimedia inputs.
Image dimension standardisation must be agreed before publishing at scale, including OMDS aspect ratios, standard form points and repository ownership.
The OMDS image repository / custom asset library must be confirmed as the source for approved images, including whether assets already used by Brent can be reused.
Criteria for switching off legacy pages must be defined and tied to the URL audit, redirect plan, testing evidence and business sign-off.
Publishing effort must include remediation for hardcoded content, content carousel decisions and template/component changes that affect page build complexity.
Cluster access and product SME content are explicit publishing dependencies and should not be left to late-stage content chasing.

Final Integrated Updates - Programme Considerations from Design, Content and Design Systems
Lifecycle and Progress Control
The programme needs a single interconnected lifecycle with a visible progress tracker covering Define, Discovery, Design, Approval, Handover, Support and Go Live.
The tracker should show workplan, milestones, assumptions, dependencies and impact, not just percentage completion.
Beta / staging readiness must be linked to design readiness, content readiness, DS readiness, platform build readiness and publishing readiness.
Non-Migrated Sites and Rollout Approach
Non-migrated sites require explicit change expectations so stakeholders understand what will change now, what will remain as-is, and what will move later.
A rollout / demo approach is required to show the target experience, validate enhanced components and manage expectation across existing live journeys.
Enhanced components may impact the list of sites in scope; this must be impact-assessed before delivery baselines are locked.
SEO, Brand and Existing Live Estate
SEO is not ready and must be treated as a blocking readiness item for page build, handover and go-live validation.
Brand interim approval is required where full CI decisions are still pending but build work needs to progress.
Ongoing new build on existing live journeys must be governed to avoid design drift, duplicated effort and inconsistent user experience.
3. Design and Design System Workstream
Area / owner: Design / Design System / OMDS
Summary
The Design System is positioned as an enterprise digital experience framework rather than only a UI component library. It governs standards, patterns, information architecture, navigation, audience journeys, templates, content standards, approvals and alignment to OMDS.
Key Notes
The delivery lifecycle runs through Requirements, Discovery, Design, Approval, Handover and Support/Publishing.
Discovery includes analytics, research, stakeholder engagement, consolidation, SEO, copy, ecosystem review, product, compliance, brand, functional analysis and OMDS input.
Design deliverables include desktop, mobile, copy readiness, SEO readiness, stakeholder review, DS review, technical review, testing and functional analysis.
Approval requires Group Marketing, internal stakeholders, external stakeholders and clusters.
Handover requires design ready, DS approval, final copy, sign-offs and rules.
Design foundations include IA and navigation containers, generic audience journeys, templates, landing pages, categories, product pages, utility pages and content standards.
Journey principles include findability, intent, access to product information, flow and page principles.
Key Risks
OMDS may be treated as a UI library rather than an enterprise governance framework.
Country-specific deviations could increase without formal exception governance.
Approval bottlenecks could slow delivery.
Content and SEO readiness may be handled too late.
Component sprawl and design debt may increase without lifecycle control.
Recommended Actions
Formalise an OMDS operating model and design authority charter.
Define component lifecycle management: proposed, approved, deprecated and retired.
Create a template catalogue covering landing, category, product and utility pages.
Document accessibility, analytics and performance standards within the design system.
Establish approval SLAs and an exception process for country-level deviations.

Final Integrated Updates - Design, Content and Design Systems
End-to-End Design Lifecycle
Define: capture the brief, scope, problem statement and required stakeholder understanding before discovery starts.
Discovery: include analytics, research, stakeholder engagement, consolidation, SEO, copy, ecosystem review, product input, compliance, brand, functional analysis, product marketing and OMDS input.
Design: produce desktop and mobile designs with copy readiness, SEO readiness, stakeholder review, DS review, technical review, testing, functional analysis and content validation.
Approval: route through Group Marketing, internal stakeholders, external stakeholders and clusters where applicable.
Handover: design ready, DS approval, final copy, SEO input, sign-offs and publishing/build rules must be packaged for DS, Web Dev and Publish / Gather teams.
Support: continuous QA, updates, final sign-offs, SEO sign-off, copy validation and DS validation must be managed before Go Live.
Go Live: should only proceed once design, content, DS, SEO, build, publishing and support acceptance criteria are satisfied.
Design System Foundations and Governance
Standards must define rules, patterns, URL rules and reusable experience principles; where notes reference "not templates", the distinction between patterns and templates must be clarified.
Governance must include approvals, alignment to OMDS, feedback / review cycles and Head of Design approval authority.
DS standards must align with Group Marketing, DS, internal stakeholders and user testing inputs.
DS design involvement must cover discovery, ideation and design, but the boundary between pure DS design and functional product build must be explicitly agreed.
Template, IA and Journey Model
Information Architecture and navigation containers remain foundational and should be validated before template scaling.
Generic journeys and audience models must drive landing pages, sections, categories, product pages and utility pages.
Templates must be structural, close IA gaps, improve findability and support common page types: landing, category, product and utility pages.
Utility templates must cover Contact Us, About Us and calculators, with product-specific variants where required.
Journey principles must explicitly test findability, intent, access to product information, flow and page-level usability principles.
Audience segmentation should support personal, insurance and investment journeys where relevant.
Content and Publishing Alignment Within Design
Content standards must sit inside the design system and govern copy structure, images, links, downloadable assets, metadata and SEO fields.
Content and product messaging must be validated before handover; late content changes will create publishing rework.
Access to content from clusters and product SMEs is a dependency for design sign-off, not only publishing execution.
Hardcoded content must be identified during design and component review so that migration does not replicate non-governed content structures.
Design System Build and Platform Build Split
DS Build should be tracked separately from Platform Build to avoid masking component readiness under general build progress.
Platform Build / Dev Build must include Contentstack setup, content, gathering, publishing and URL readiness.
Product messaging and stakeholder journeys should be validated against both DS Build and Platform Build before approval.
Updated Design / DS Risks
Capacity risk remains high across publishing, copywriting and design volume.
There is currently no clear capacity to support the full scope if design, DS, content and publishing workstreams scale in parallel.
Brand / CI pressure may force interim decisions; Wealth CI may need to be treated as a separate platform / experience until governance is clarified.
Dedicated capacity from clusters is required; otherwise content and approval bottlenecks will shift downstream into publishing and testing.
Enhanced components may expand the impacted site list and should be assessed before the workplan is baselined.

Summary
The execution discussion confirms that the migration is not simply a content move. It includes content migration, Design System adoption, technical uplift, IA improvements, new components, analytics, observability and environment sequencing. Key Notes

6. Content Governance and Migration Readiness
Area / owner: Content / Publishing / Design System / SEO
Summary
The final design, content and design system update confirms that content readiness is a programme control point. Content is not simply moved at the end: it must be prepared, governed, mapped, approved and packaged before design handover, publishing and go-live decisions can be accepted.
Key Notes
All current URLs must be mapped to the new world through a formal URL audit before page switch-off or redirect decisions are made.
As-is pages must be defined before migration estimates, publishing effort and testing scope are baselined.
Content packs to publishers must include copy, images, link mapping, PDFs / downloadable assets, image dimensions, aspect ratios and any multimedia inputs.
Image standards should be governed through OMDS, including aspect ratios, form points and repository ownership.
Article page treatment, content carousel usage, landing page ownership and theming decisions remain design/content decisions that affect build and publishing effort.
SEO readiness must be embedded in Discovery, Design, Handover and Support; it is currently not ready and should be tracked as a blocker.
Hardcoded content must be audited and remediated rather than simply migrated.
Criteria for legacy page switch-off must be tied to URL mapping, content sign-off, QA evidence, SEO validation and business approval.
Required Content Gates
Discovery gate: content owner, product SME, cluster input, SEO input and analytics input identified.
Design gate: copy ready, SEO ready, image standards known, content model confirmed and hardcoded content flagged.
Approval gate: business, brand, compliance, cluster and DS approvals complete where applicable.
Handover gate: final content pack, URL mapping, rules and sign-offs provided to DS, Web Dev and Publish / Gather.
Support / Go-live gate: QA, updates, sign-off, SEO sign-off, copy validation, redirects and switch-off criteria completed.
Content Risks
Late content ownership decisions will delay design approval and publishing.
Unresolved SEO readiness will cause rework after build and may block go-live.
Unstandardised images and assets will create inconsistent pages and publishing delays.
Undefined switch-off criteria will create risk around legacy decommissioning and broken customer journeys.
Cluster capacity constraints may block content validation, sign-off and localisation.
Recommended Actions
Create a content readiness checklist and enforce it as a formal entry criterion for design handover and publishing.
Assign owners for landing pages, article pages, content carousel decisions and theming decisions.
Create an asset standards pack covering image dimensions, aspect ratios, repositories and reuse rules.
Complete the URL audit and align it to redirects, switch-off criteria and SEO validation.
Add content readiness, SEO readiness and asset readiness to the programme progress tracker.
